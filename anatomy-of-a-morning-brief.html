What a good morning brief actually contains

Most reports are an inventory. A brief is an argument. Here's the anatomy of the one Adgent writes every morning — and the rule that decides what gets left out.

A
The Adgent team
Notes from building the analyst

There's a difference between a report and a brief, and it's not length. A report tells you what happened. A brief tells you what to do about what happened — and stakes its reputation on the difference.

One: the narrative line

The first thing in the brief is a sentence, not a chart. "Account's steady. One thing needs you: the retargeting creative is done."

This is the part that has to survive being read on a phone in a lift. If the whole brief were deleted except that line, you should still make the right call today. Everything below it exists to justify it — not to compete with it for your attention.

Two: the health read

Then a single read on the account's state, with the reason attached. Not a score floating without context — a claim you can argue with. An unexplained number is a rumour.

The health read exists to answer "is this a normal day?" fast enough that on the 80% of days when it is, you stop reading and get on with your work. A brief that demands your full attention every day has failed at its job.

Most mornings, the right output is "nothing needs you." A tool that can't say that will invent work to justify itself.

Three: the items, ranked by impact

Underneath: the things that actually need a decision, in the order money says they matter. Each one carries the same four things:

  • What changed — the observation, in plain language, with the number that shows it.
  • Why it matters — the euros at stake, so you can tell a rounding error from a real problem.
  • What we'd do — a specific proposed action, not a suggestion to "consider optimizing."
  • Why we're confident — the evidence, including whether the number is mature enough to act on.

That last one is what separates a brief from a guess. Every item says how sure it is and why — and anything still inside its attribution window gets flagged rather than acted on.

The rule that shapes all of it

If it didn't change the conclusion, it isn't in the brief.

That's the whole editorial policy. Your CTR held steady — that's not an item, it's a non-event. Spend was 3% over plan — that's a rounding error, not news. The retargeting creative crossed a fatigue threshold on €2,400 of weekly spend — that's the brief.

Everything else is still there. Ask and it'll show you any number in the account, broken down however you like. It just won't put it in front of you unbidden, because the scarce resource in your morning isn't data. It's attention — and a brief that spends yours carelessly is just a dashboard with better manners.
